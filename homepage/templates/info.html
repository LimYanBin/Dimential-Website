How common is dementia?

December 8, 2022 by NIH

Dementia is the loss of cognitive functioning — thinking, remembering, and reasoning — to such an extent that it interferes with a person's daily life and activities.

Dementia affects millions of people and is more common as people grow older (about one-third of all people age 85 or older may have some form of dementia) but it is not a normal part of aging. Many people live into their 90s and beyond without any signs of dementia.

Statistic had shown that at least 65 years of age, there is an estimated 5.0 million adults with dementia in 2014 and projected to be nearly 14 million by 2060.

What increases the risk for dementia?

April 5, 2019 by CDC

  • Age

    • The strongest known risk factor for dementia is increasing age, with most cases affecting those of 65 years and older.

  • Family history

    • Those who have parents or siblings with dementia are more likely to develop dementia themselves.

  • Race/ethnicity

    • Older African Americans are twice more likely to have dementia than whites. Hispanics 1.5 times more likely to have dementia than whites.

  • Poor heart health

    • High blood pressure, high cholesterol, and smoking increase the risk of dementia if not treated properly.

  • Traumatic brain injury

    • Head injuries can increase the risk of dementia, especially if they are severe or occur repeatedly.

Difference between normal memory problems and dementia

October 21, 2020 by NIH

There are different forms of dementia, Alzheimer's disease is the most common form in people over age 65. The table below explains some differences between normal signs of aging and Alzheimer's.

Comparison between Normal Aging and Alzheimer's Disease
        Normal Aging       Alzheimer's Disease
Making a bad decision once in a while Making poor judgments and decisions a lot of the time
Missing a monthly payment Problems taking care of monthly bills
Forgetting which day it is and remembering it later Losing track of the date or time of year
Sometimes forgetting which word to use Trouble having a conversation
Losing things from time to time Misplacing things often and being unable to find them

Tips for dealing with forgetfulness
People with some forgetfulness can use a variety of techniques that
may help them stay healthy and deal with changes in their memory
and mental skills. Here are some tips:

  • Follow a daily routine.
  • Don't drink a lot of alcohol.
  • Spend time with friends and family.
  • Prevent or control high blood pressure.
  • Put your wallet or purse, keys, phone, and glasses in the same place each day.
